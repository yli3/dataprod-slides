openFDA Enforcement Data

openFDA is an open data initiative by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. Among the endpoints it provides is Enforcement Data, which records and classifies recalls of food products monitored by the FDA since 2012.

Our aim will be to explore, on a per-state and national basis, the frequency of such reports over time. To that end, we have created a Shiny app using data compiled in 2015 October. U.S. national data is depicted below:

Food Product Recalls

According to the FDA, a recall is the most effective way of addressing a public health hazard posed by an FDA-regulated product. They are "almost always" voluntary, but may be suggested or even mandated in rare cases1.

The FDA monitors and advises the recall process, and classifies cases based on the degree of the adverse health effects possible or likely to be posed2:

  • Class I - a potential for serious or irreversible effects.
  • Class II - a potential for temporary or reversible effects, with more serious consequences being unlikely.
  • Class III - not likely to cause adverse health consequences, but nonetheless a product in violation.

When a recall occurs, the FDA classifies and publishes the incident record.

FDA reports are published weekly at http://www.fda.gov/safety/recalls/enforcementreports/. You may subscribe to updates!

Data API

Data is provided in JSON format via an API endpoint3 that allows for search parameters (to isolate by state or class as required). We use the count parameter to generate timeseries data, in order to obtain a big picture summary of enforcement history.

https://api.fda.gov/food/enforcement.json?search=classification:%22Class%20III%22+AND+state:%22CA%22&count=report_date

We further make use of the lubridate package and data.table grammar to repackage the obtained data on a monthly basis. The goal is to visualize, for different states and classifications, the total number of recalls issued each month.

In our app, we compile data acquired via this API in October of 2015, and explore these trends with ggplot. Users may select both state and class, or observe national-level data.

Visualize

For demonstration, we put it all together with Class I recall data from California. Try it with your state in our online Shiny app!

  # Get data.

  # Format data.
  d$time <- as.Date(d$time, "%Y%m%d")
  d[, month := month(time)]
  d[, year := year(time)]
  d <- d[, .(monthlyCount = sum(count)), by = .(month, year)]
  d$monthlyTime <- as.Date(paste(d$month, "1", d$year, sep = "-"), format = "%m-%d-%Y")

  # Plot data.
  qplot(monthlyTime, monthlyCount, d, geom = "line")
